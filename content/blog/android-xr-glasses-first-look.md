Google finally shipped consumer AR glasses with Samsung and Warby Parker. I got my hands on a pair and have been wearing them for a week. Here's my honest take.

## The Hardware

They look like normal glasses. That's the whole point. Warby Parker's design influence is obvious — I've gotten compliments on them, and no one realized they're smart glasses unless I pointed it out. The frames come in five styles, and prescription lenses are supported.

## What They Do Well

- **Turn-by-turn navigation** projected in your peripheral vision — genuinely useful
- **Notifications** that don't scream for attention; they sit quietly until you look
- **Live captions** for conversations — game-changing for anyone with hearing difficulties
- **Google Lens integration** — point at a landmark, get info instantly

## What Needs Work

Battery life is about 4 hours with active use. The field of view is still narrow — 30 degrees, which feels like watching a movie through a mail slot. And at $399, they're competing with a lot of other things you could buy.

## The Verdict

This is the first AR product I'd actually recommend to a non-tech person. Not because it's perfect — it's not. But because it *disappears*. You forget you're wearing them. That's the magic.

Android XR as a platform matters more than the glasses themselves. If Google plays this like Android — open, licensable, multiple OEMs — they could actually win AR where everyone else fumbled.

I just hope they don't kill it in two years like every other Google hardware product.
