## When Does a Frame Stop Being a Frame?

DLSS 5 was announced at GTC 2026 and honestly, I'm equal parts impressed and philosophically conflicted. NVIDIA claims it can generate up to 7 interpolated frames for every real rendered frame. Seven.

Let me be clear: the tech is astonishing. The new transformer-based frame generation model is light-years ahead of the CNN approach in DLSS 3. I've seen the demo running on a Vera Rubin RTX card — Cyberpunk 2078 at 480 fps on a 4K OLED. The motion clarity is legitimately good. No ghosting, no shimmer on thin geometry.

But here's where I start to push back. At what point does "rendering" stop being about the renderer and start being about the AI's interpretation? DLSS 1.0 was upscaling. DLSS 3 added frame gen. DLSS 5 is basically having the GPU dream most of the frames while you're playing.

For competitive gamers, latency is still the elephant in the room. Reflex 3.0 helps, but interpolated frames are inherently delayed. Single-player experiences? This is incredible. Esports? I'm skeptical.

Still, I can't deny the sheer engineering achievement. The new motion vector model handles fast camera pans and particle effects better than native rendering at lower settings. NVIDIA is betting the future of real-time graphics on generative AI, and DLSS 5 is the most audacious example yet.
