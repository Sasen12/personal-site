Conversational AI editing in Google Photos has started rolling out internationally, and I've been using it for two weeks through a VPN workaround. Now that it's officially here, I can share my thoughts.

## How It Works

You open a photo and tap the new Gemini button. Instead of manual sliders, you *describe what you want*:

- "Make the sky more dramatic"
- "Remove that trash can in the corner"
- "Make everyone's faces brighter"
- "Turn this into a cinematic black and white"

The AI understands natural language and applies the edits as layered masks — not destructive changes. You can tweak individual adjustments afterward.

## Where It Shines

Group photos are the killer use case. "Fix closed eyes" detected the one person blinking in a group shot and replaced their face with a better frame from a burst. It took three seconds. The "best take" feature from previous Pixels was already impressive, but this is next-level.

## Where It Struggles

Complex requests like "make this look like a Wes Anderson film" are hit or miss. The model understands the reference but doesn't have a clear visual target. Simpler, more concrete instructions work better.

## The Bigger Picture

This is the direction all creative tools are heading. The interface is shifting from knobs and sliders to language and intent. Adobe's been moving this way too, but Google's advantage is integration — the AI lives inside Photos, not a separate app.

My Prediction: within two years, we'll look at manual photo editing the way we look at command-line FTP. Powerful, but why would you when you can just say what you want?

The future of creativity is conversational. And I'm here for it.
