Apple announced a significant overhaul of parental controls and child safety features at WWDC, and I have complicated feelings about it. On one hand, the new tools are genuinely powerful. On the other hand, I'm watching the same people who demanded these features now worry about "Apple tracking children."

## What They Announced

The new system is built around what Apple calls "Age Appropriate Design." Instead of blanket content filters, it adapts protections based on age ranges and individual maturity:

- **Communication Safety 2.0** extends to third-party apps through a system-level API. Any app that wants App Store distribution has to integrate it. Sensitive content detection now works across Messages, AirDrop, FaceTime, and participating third-party platforms.

- **Screen Distance+** uses the front-facing sensors to detect when a child's face is too close to the screen for too long, and intelligently suggests breaks. It's opt-in and all data stays on-device.

- **Purchase Delegation** lets parents approve spending through a frictionless "ask to buy" flow that even works with physical Apple Pay transactions.

## My Take

I think this is genuinely well-designed. The key insight is that everything processes on-device — no cloud uploads, no back-end machine learning on children's data. Apple's privacy stance here is consistent: protect the child without surveilling them.

The cynic in me notes that this also strengthens Apple's argument against third-party app stores and sideloading. "Why would you need external app sources when our ecosystem has built-in child safety?" It's a convenient regulatory shield.

But as a tool for actual parents? This is the best implementation I've seen. The old Screen Time was a joke — my nephew could bypass it in about 30 seconds. The new system uses parent-managed accounts with real enforcement. It'll be interesting to see how third-party messaging apps handle the Communication Safety API mandate.
