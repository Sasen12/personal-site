The rumor mill lit up this week: Apple is reportedly in talks with Samsung to produce future A-series and M-series chips at Samsung's Texas fab. On the surface, this sounds wild — Apple and Samsung are fierce rivals in phones, displays, and basically everything else. But dig deeper and it starts to make uncomfortable sense.

## Why Now

Taiwan's geopolitical situation isn't getting more stable. TSMC's fabs are overwhelmingly concentrated in a region that China considers part of its territory. For a company that sells $400 billion worth of products a year, having 90% of your most critical component manufactured in a single geopolitical hotspot is not a strategy — it's a gamble.

Samsung's Taylor, Texas fab is ramping up 3nm and soon 2nm产能. It's not TSMC-level yields yet, but it's getting there. Splitting production between TSMC (Taiwan) and Samsung (US) gives Apple a genuine hedge. The US government's CHIPS Act subsidies sweeten the deal considerably.

## The Catch

Samsung's foundry has historically had yield problems and power efficiency that trails TSMC by a generation. If Apple compromises on chip quality just to diversify supply, that's a real loss for consumers. An M6 that's 15% less efficient because it's built on Samsung's process would be felt in battery life and thermals.

I think the most likely outcome is Apple uses Samsung for older-generation chips (A-series for base iPhones, maybe M-series for iPads) while keeping TSMC for the flagship silicon. Either way, this is a massive deal. Apple hasn't seriously dual-sourced chips since the iPhone 6S era. If this goes through, it reshapes the entire semiconductor landscape.
